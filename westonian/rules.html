-ALL verbs are followed by -er. If any verb is not marked with -er on this spreadsheet, it is an error
-If two vowels are together that are not described on the alphabet chart as making a specific sound when placed together, they are ALWAYS pronounced separately.
-When two conflicting obstruents like t and d are together, in a combination such as "dt," they are pronounced as by default in English.
-en is ALWAYS the plural suffix. If a suffix starts with a vowel and the word it is modifying ends with a vowel, in a case in which the combination of those vowels is able to create a specific sound as described on the alphabet chart, they are always pronounced separately.
-The act of being something overrides the act of doing something (verb). For example, to work is "laborer." For a person to work, it is "der persone laborer." However, a worker is not called a "laborerist," they are called a "laborist," as "ist" replaces "er." However, to be overworked is "exerlaborer," because the overriding only happens when there is another suffix present (all to say this rule does not apply for prefixes). Another example would be, "I am working": "Is eim laborieon." The act of being suffix overrides the act of doing suffix.
-Verbs retain their verb suffix (er) when being described in any past, present or future tense. (but the same rule applies when suffixes need to be replaced (see above))
-In most cases, the tense phrase can be dropped when it has already been used once in a sentence. For example, in the sentence "Is brüder ad cogner is eim yin donkey" (my brother thought I was a donkey), it is clear that the second verb (was) is without a tense phrase after it. This is because we already know the tense of the sentence because the tense phrase was already used previously for the verb "cogner" (think). However, if the user wants to convey a more specific meaning, it might be important not to omit the tense phrase.
-Westonian is a language that is intended for the purpose of getting the point across, without any extra flourishes or unneccesary words. That being said, word choice must be carefully selected in order to make the clause of a sentence apparent. Since there are so few words in the language's dictionary, some sentences can have multiple meanings (especially since certain words, like "Is" can mean multiple things, in this case "I, me, mine, etc.). The language relies heavily on the user's creativity to communicate these things. There are fewer standards for communication in this language than some other languages, so grammar and sentence structure are a bit more loose. Again, most of these things besides things that are explicitly laid out in this column are totally up to the user's interpretation.
-Word order in Westonian can be used the exact same or very similarly to English. It is up to the creativity of the user for sentences that can be paraphrased. An example would be the sentence, "I will not go to your house." This could be phrased, "Is nach möter auf sü haus," (I not go in your house) or, "möter auf su haus is nach." (go in your house I will not). Both make sense in English, so they should make sense translated in our out of Westonian.
-Certain words that may not really be necessary to the main point of the sentence are not required to be used in the sentence, and it is actually discouraged strongly against for anyone trying to learn the language as often these extra words are unneccesary. Speakers using the translator will notice that these words are included in the translated sentence, because the translator is not always 100% accurate. This approach is technically correct, but advised against for the sake of simplicity.
-Oust suffix replaces Ous suffix, and this applies with other suffixes that are the same or sound similar. Whatever makes the word sound good and make sense.
-Verbs used in sentences like, "Made in China," where there is not enough context to detemine the tense, will be followed by the suffix "at." This also applies to sentences like "This is being reverse engineered," in which the tense is obviously present, but the verb appears to be in the past tense.
-Many verbs can be changed into an adjective or noun by removing the "r" at the end. As an example, the sentence "That would be my guess," would be written as, "Das wout deu is's cogele" (In this instance, the verb form "cogeler" is replaced with "cogele." -Likewise, many nouns can be changed into a verb by using this process in the reverse order.
-When a past or present tense is used, the "er" at the end of a verb CAN be removed (although it is not mandatory).
